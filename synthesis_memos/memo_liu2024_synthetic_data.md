# Common-Reading Memo: Liu et al. 2024

**Paper:** *Best Practices and Lessons Learned on Synthetic Data for Language Models*
**Authors:** Ruibo Liu, Jerry Wei, Fangyu Liu, Chenglei Si, Yanzhe Zhang, Jinmeng Rao, Nathan Scales, Noah Fiedel, Denny Zhou, Diyi Yang
**Venue:** COLM 2024
**Read by:** Mistire Daniel — 2026-04-28

---

## Summary

Liu et al. survey the landscape of synthetic data for LLM training and evaluation, drawing on 50+ papers across pretraining, alignment, and reasoning tasks. Their central claim is that synthetic data quality matters more than quantity: a carefully filtered 10K synthetic set consistently outperforms 100K unfiltered examples. They recommend three best practices: (1) diverse seed prompts with high-temperature decoding to maximize coverage, (2) a separate, stronger judge model for quality filtering, and (3) iterative self-play or back-translation to close the distribution gap between synthetic and real data.

The paper also warns about a "synthetic data collapse" failure mode: if the same model family generates and judges, the synthetic distribution narrows toward that family's prior, ultimately harming performance on out-of-distribution inputs.

---

## Where Tenacious-Bench Agrees

**Quality over quantity — confirmed.** Our judge filter (IC ≥ 4, GV ≥ 4, RC ≥ 4 on a 1–5 scale) enforced this directly: 2 of 20 synthesis seeds were rejected (90% pass rate) because they produced vague `fail_patterns` that could not be mechanically verified. Those 2 rejected tasks had higher surface diversity than some accepted tasks but were excluded on quality grounds. The final 18-task synthesis set is tighter and more actionable than the 20-seed raw set.

**Separate judge model — implemented.** `judge_filter.py` uses DeepSeek V3-0324 to score outputs generated by Qwen3-235B. This is the Liu et al. recommendation applied directly, and it also satisfies the Li et al. 2025 preference-leakage prevention requirement.

---

## Where Tenacious-Bench Disagrees

**Disagreement: high-temperature decoding for seed diversity is wrong for evaluation datasets.**

Liu et al. recommend temperature=0.9–1.2 for the *generator* to maximize coverage. We used temperature=0.7 for Qwen3 generation and temperature=0.3 for DeepSeek judgment. The paper justifies high temperature by pointing to pretraining and SFT use cases where diverse coverage is the primary goal.

For Tenacious-Bench, the primary goal is *rubric precision*, not surface diversity. A benchmark task with high-temperature paraphrasing of the same adversarial instruction is not more useful — it is noisier. The five failure families have specific trigger conditions (exact `fail_patterns`, exact threshold values) that must be preserved through generation. High temperature introduces stochastic paraphrases that may land on or off the trigger condition unpredictably.

**Evidence from our data:** The 2 rejected synthesis tasks (out of 20) were both high-temperature outputs. Both produced `fail_patterns` that were vague synonyms of the trigger phrase (e.g., "aggressively growing" instead of "scaling aggressively") — close enough to the intent but not mechanically verifiable by the `scoring_evaluator.py` regex layer. The 18 accepted tasks were all generated with temperature ≤ 0.7 or used explicit template constraints.

**Conclusion:** For evaluation benchmark construction, the Liu et al. diversity-through-temperature recommendation should be replaced with diversity-through-seed-design. Cover edge cases by authoring more probe seeds, not by sampling the same seed at higher temperature.

## Application to Tenacious-Bench v0.2

The one Liu et al. finding we plan to adopt more aggressively is **back-translation for distribution gap closure**: generating a candidate output, having a judge critique it, then using the critique as a new prompt to generate a harder variant. This would allow F1/F4 adversarial tasks to be systematically strengthened without manual authoring — a potential path to 300+ tasks in Act IV without exponential human effort.
